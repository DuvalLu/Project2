Medium Breeds

Medium-Breed Dog Information and Attributes Medium-sized dogs are generally defined as those weighing between 20–60 pounds and standing 15–25 inches tall at the shoulder, though exact ranges vary by breed and kennel classification petandlife.com+1. They strike a balance between small and large breeds, offering affection, manageability, and versatility. Key Characteristics Size & Build: Compact yet sturdy, with moderate energy levels — more relaxed than small breeds but not as high-strung as large working dogs petandlife.com+1. Temperament: Often friendly, loyal, and trainable; some are calm (e.g., Bulldog, Whippet) while others are energetic (e.g., Brittany, Shetland Sheepdog) American Kennel Club. Versatility: Found in all major groups — herding (Border Collie, Australian Shepherd), sporting (Beagle, English Springer Spaniel), working (Airedale Terrier, Appenzeller), and companion (Cocker Spaniel, American Water Spaniel) petandlife.com. Lifestyle Fit: Adapt well to apartments, houses, and families; can thrive with or without a yard if exercise needs are met petandlife.com+1. Grooming & Care: Easier to groom and handle than large breeds; coat types range from short to long, silky to corded American Kennel Club. Common Medium-Breed Examples Airedale Terrier – 50–70 lbs, 21–23 in; bold, intelligent, versatile Canine Bible. American English Coonhound – 20–30 lbs, 13.5–15.5 in; affectionate, good with kids petandlife.com+1. American Staffordshire Terrier – 24–38 lbs, 14–16 in; loyal, courageous www.dogpackapp.com. Australian Cattle Dog – 30–50 lbs, 17–20 in; strong, intelligent herder petandlife.com+1. Basset Hound – 36–40 lbs, 12–15 in; calm, affectionate, good with families Canine Bible. English Springer Spaniel – 40–50 lbs, 19–20 in; energetic, playful Canine Bible. Whippet – 25–40 lbs, 18–22 in; athletic, calm-tempered Canine Bible+1. Care & Ownership Considerations Exercise: Moderate; daily walks and play are usually sufficient petandlife.com. Training: Generally quick learners; some may be stubborn, so early socialization is key American Kennel Club. Health: Common issues vary by breed (e.g., hip dysplasia in some herding breeds, eye problems in some hounds) petandlife.com. Cost: Lower maintenance than large breeds; food, grooming, and vet care are manageable. Bottom line: Medium breeds are ideal for owners seeking a dog that’s not too big for space or too small for energy, with a mix of companionship and work ability. Choosing the right breed depends on your lifestyle, activity level, and home environment.
